WHAT DID YOU JUST WITNESS?

Climate hazards are naturally occurring throughout the world in weather cycles. Natural disasters are normal and has been occurring since the world began. Droughts, floods, bushfires, hurricanes all start naturally however as of 2021 we are witnessing destruction beyond the natural means of the world.

In recent years especially after the industrial revolution there have been a series of devastating weather events. According to the World Meteorological Organisation the number of disasters has increased by a factor of five in half a century, further driven by climate change more and more extreme weather events are occurring and being reported. Although natural disasters have been happening the severity of these disasters are exponentially increasing due to climate change.

How does climate change worsen natural disasters?

According to the United States Geological Survey (USGS), a government organisation that monitors, assess and conducts targeted science research. With the increasing global surface temperatures caused by climate change the possibility of more droughts and increased intensity of storms will likely occur. As more water vapour is evaporated into the atmosphere it becomes a fuel for more storms to develop. More heat in the atmosphere and warmer ocean surface temperatures can lead to increased wind speeds in tropical storms. This means that although the number of natural disasters remain constant the intensity and destructiveness of those are increasing.

WHAT IS CLIMATE CHANGE?

Climate change is defined as a significant change of average weather conditions for example getting warming, wetter or drier over decades. Although this does not impact us instantly it is the longer term trend that is concerning. Our globe naturally goes through carbon absorbing phases long before humans were around. This is where the carbon dioxide levels rises and gets absorbed by natural means, this can include trees, plants and other natural greens to help regulate CO2 levels. However since the industrial revolution when coal burning got introduced for manufacturing the world got introduced to carbon dioxide emissions like never seen before.

The main driver for climate change is the greenhouse effect.

As humans produce more and more greenhouse gases (methane, nitrous oxide and most prominently carbon dioxide) it creates an ozone layer that traps the natural heat produced by the sun. These heat waves are usually reflected back or absorbed naturally through photo synthesis and other natural processes of our plants and forests. However with the exponential continuous increase of green house gases during the industrial revolution the ozone layer has become so thick that heat from the sun is trapping heat within the atmosphere.

Humans are increasingly influencing the climate and Earth's temperature by our daily activities; burning fossil fuels, deforestation and farming livestock. Since the industrial revolution burning coal, oil and gas has been dramatically increased for production and manufacturing.

Burning of fossil fuel for energy

When fossil fuels are burned, they release large amounts of carbon dioxide, a greenhouse gas into the air. Greenhouse gases then continue to trap heat in our atmosphere through the greenhouse effect causing global warming.

Transportation

According to Climate Council Australia transport is Australia's third largest source of greenhouse gas emissions with the highest rate of growth totalling to 17% of Australia's total emissions. Collectively, cars in Australia emit the same amount of pollution as Queensland's entire fossil fuelled electricity supply.

Deforestation

Clearing of forests affects the local temperatures and precipitation patterns as it reduces rainfall and disrupts the natural water cycle. The act of deforestation alone emits large amounts of carbon dioxide into the air but the most concerning consequence is trees take up atmospheric carbon dioxide and converts that to energy and oxygen.

Food and agriculture

In 2019, agriculture alone produced 13% of Australia's greenhouse gases. Activities such as deforestation for agricultural lands and emissions of greenhouse gases through the enteric fermentation of ruminant livestock has detrimental effects to global warming. Cows and other livestock contribute 37% of human activity related methane emissions (More potent greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide).
